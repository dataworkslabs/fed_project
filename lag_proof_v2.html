The Fed's Variance Problem

Interest rate changes take 12–18 months to affect the real economy. That delay doesn't just make the Fed's job harder — it increases the variance of outcomes. Even a correct decision today can produce wildly different results depending on what happens in those 18 months. That's what makes discretionary policy fragile.

The lag increases the variance of outcomes, which makes discretionary policy more fragile than rule-based or fiscal-coordinated frameworks. Even a perfect Fed with perfect intentions cannot eliminate this structural uncertainty.
⚠️

An honest note before we begin: The Fed is not solely responsible for the crises below. Housing bubble causes included regulatory failures, Wall Street securitization, and rating agency fraud. COVID inflation was driven partly by $5 trillion in fiscal stimulus and global supply shocks. But in every case, the Fed's 12–18 month lag made a bad situation worse — either by acting too late, stopping too early, or overcorrecting. That's the argument.

Cycle 1 — Dot-Com Era · 2001–2004 Low Rates Too Long — But Not the Only Reason
Lag argument: Moderate
2000–2001 Dot-com collapses + 9/11 Dual shock. Deflation risk real.
Fed acts
2001–2003 Rates cut to 1% Fear of deflation. Japan's 1990s loomed large.
⏱ ~18mo lag
2003 Economy recovers GDP growing. But lag meant rates stayed low.
unintended fuel
2004–2006 Housing bubble inflates Low rates: one ingredient among many.
📍 The lag argument
The Fed kept rates at 1% until mid-2004 — a full year after GDP had clearly recovered. The lag in data made it hard to know when "enough was enough." That extra year of cheap money was fuel for housing speculation.
⚖️ The fair counterargument
Low rates weren't the housing bubble's only cause. Wall Street securitization, NINJA loans, rating agency fraud, and lax regulation all played major roles. The Fed was one factor — not the whole story. Greenspan later admitted this was a mistake, but it was complicated.
Cycle 2 — Financial Crisis · 2006–2010 Hikes Worked — Then the System Broke Anyway
Lag argument: Moderate
2004–2006 Fed raises rates steadily 17 consecutive hikes. Intentional tightening.
⏱ lag bites hard
2007–2008 Housing collapses + Lehman fails Fragile system implodes. Not just the rates.
emergency triage
2008 Fed cuts to 0% Necessary — system was seizing up.
⏱ slow recovery
2008–2015 Zero rates for 7 years Unemployment stayed high. Inflation below target. Rates justified.
📍 The lag argument
The Fed's 2004–2006 hikes were already working — but the lag meant the data looked bad even as the medicine took effect. The system's fragility amplified what should have been a manageable correction into a full collapse.
⚖️ The fair counterargument
Zero rates for 7 years didn't cause runaway inflation — which actually weakens a simple "low rates = bubbles" story. The real economy was genuinely weak 2008–2015. The crisis was caused by leverage and opacity in the financial system, not just rate timing. Keeping rates at zero was arguably correct given the data.
Cycle 3 — COVID Era · 2020–2022 The Clearest Case: "Transitory" Was Wrong
Lag argument: Strongest
Mar 2020 COVID shock Economy shuts down. Emergency response needed.
same-day response
Mar 2020 Fed cuts to 0% + $120B/month asset purchases. Appropriate.
⏱ lag + forecasting error
Mid-2021 Economy fully recovered Labor market ripping. Fed still at zero — called inflation "transitory."
too late to act
Jun 2022 9.1% inflation Worst in 40 years. Ordinary Americans paid the price.
📍 The lag argument — strongest here
By mid-2021, the data was clear: recovery was complete. But the Fed waited until March 2022 to raise rates — nearly two full years after cutting to zero. The lag plus a forecasting error ("transitory") meant they were 12+ months behind. That delay directly contributed to the worst inflation in a generation.
⚖️ The fair counterargument
The inflation wasn't purely a Fed mistake. $5 trillion in fiscal stimulus (CARES + ARP), global supply chain collapse, and an energy shock all contributed. But the Fed's delayed response made it significantly worse — and this time, unlike 2008–2015, they genuinely misread the signals. The lag made a bad forecast catastrophic.
So What Does This Actually Prove?
The core problem
The lag increases variance, not just latency
The Fed might make the correct decision given available data — and still produce a bad outcome. The 18-month gap means the economy can change dramatically before the medicine works. That's not incompetence. That's structural fragility.
Why discretion makes it worse
Human judgment amplifies the lag's damage
The Taylor Rule — a mechanical formula — would have raised rates automatically in 2021, no human judgment required. The Fed's discretion to call inflation "transitory" added delay on top of the lag. Discretion + lag = fragility.
The alternative
Rules reduce variance. Discretion increases it.
Rule-based frameworks (Taylor Rule, NGDP targeting) or better fiscal-monetary coordination would reduce how much discretionary judgment is needed — and therefore how much variance the lag can introduce. Not a perfect fix. But a structurally sounder one.
⚠️

The argument isn't that the lag causes every crisis. It's that the lag increases the variance of outcomes — meaning even correct Fed decisions can produce catastrophic results depending on what happens in those 18 months. That variance is what makes discretionary policy fragile. A rule-based framework like the Taylor Rule would have raised rates in 2021 automatically — no "transitory" call, no delay, no discretion. It might still have been wrong. But it would have been wrong faster, with less accumulated damage. The dual mandate doesn't just ask the Fed to balance two goals. It asks them to balance two lagged, uncertain signals using human judgment — and then live with the variance that creates.
