Independence Is the Last Line of Defense

The Fed's tools are blunt, its mandate is contradictory, and its decisions work on a delay. Political independence doesn't fix any of that — but losing it removes even the limited ability to manage those constraints.

History shows one clear pattern: when the Fed bends to political pressure, inflation spirals. When it stays independent, it can course-correct. The threat today is the same as 1971.

Nixon told Burns directly: "We'll take inflation." Burns complied, keeping rates artificially low before the 1972 election. The result was the worst peacetime inflation in American history — proof that political capture doesn't just compromise independence, it breaks the institution's ability to function.

Volcker raised rates to 20% — knowing it would cause a recession — and held them there despite massive political pressure, protests, and death threats. He did what was right for the long term over the short term. That's only possible with genuine independence from political cycles.

⚠️

Independence doesn't fix the dual mandate's structural problems. The contradiction between inflation and unemployment goals is still there. The 18-month lag still increases variance. The blunt tool problem remains. But independence is what allows the Fed to make the right long-term call even when it's politically painful. Burns showed what happens when that's gone: both goals fail simultaneously. Volcker showed what's possible when it holds. Powell is facing the same choice right now — and the outcome will determine whether the next bubble cycle is manageable or catastrophic.
